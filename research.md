## Research Interests

My research interests span several areas of physics, with a focus on:

- **High Energy Physics**: I have been particularly interested in particle interactions, collider physics, and the application of machine learning techniques to improve event reconstruction and data analysis.
- **Machine Learning**: I explore the use of advanced machine learning algorithms, including deep learning and reinforcement learning, to tackle complex problems in physics.
- **Neuroscience**: Related to machine learning, I am interested in understanding neural computation and how insights from neuroscience can inform the development of novel algorithms.

## Current Projects

### Master's Thesis: Improving Event Reconstruction in Top Quark Pairs using Machine Learning Techniques

**Duration**: 06/04/2025 - 6/07/2026
**Supervisor**: Dr. Katharina Behr, DESY Hamburg

**Abstract**: My thesis focuses on enhancing the accuracy of event reconstruction in top quark pair production at the LHC by leveraging advanced machine learning algorithms. The goal is to improve the identification of decay products and improve measurement precision.

**Key Objectives**:
- Implement state-of-the-art ML models for event reconstruction
- Combine several aspects of the reconstruction process
- Evaluate performance against traditional methods

**Methodology**: Utilizing Python with TensorFlow and PyTorch frameworks to develop and train models on simulated LHC data.

**Preliminary Results**: Initial tests show promising improvements in reconstruction accuracy compared to baseline methods.

## Research Philosophy

I believe in the power of interdisciplinary approaches to solve complex scientific problems. By combining theoretical insights with cutting-edge computational techniques, I aim to contribute to advancements in our understanding of fundamental physics while also pushing the boundaries of machine learning applications. The current hype of machine learning is exciting, but as a physicist I am particularly interested in how these methods can be grounded in first principles and lead to interpretable results.
